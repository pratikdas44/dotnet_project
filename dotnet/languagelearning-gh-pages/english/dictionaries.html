For non-native speakers of English, a “learner's dictionary” is more useful than a dictionary aimed at native speakers. When you choose a dictionary (whether printed or digital), check that it provides usage notes (e.g. on difference between certain synonyms and near synonyms, e.g. "change", "alter" and "modify"), full sentence examples and examples of prepositions that can be used with the noun or verb you are looking up.

Monolingual Dictionaries (British English)

  • Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary. See also the online version.
  • Collins Cobuild Advanced Learner's English Dictionary. See also the online dictionary.
  • Longman Dictionary of Contemporary English. See also Longman English Dictionary Online.
  • Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary. See Cambridge Dictionary Online for an online dictionary and thesaurus.
  • Macmillan Dictionary.
  • Open Dictionary: Macmillan's crowdsourced dictionary.

Also useful:

  • The Academic Phrasebank by the University of Manchester helps you find examples of key words and phrases. Non-native speakers of English are one of the primary audiences of this resource.

Monolingual Dictionaries (Other Variations of English)

  • Merriam-Webster Dictionary and Thesaurus: dictionary of American English.
  • Urban Dictionary: dictionary of contempary American slang.
  • Macquarie Dictionary: dictionary of Australian English.
  • Big Huge Thesaurus (based on source data from the Princeton University WordNet database, the Carnegie Mellon Pronouncing Dictionary, and suggestions from thousands of people on the internet).
