General Information

German is a West Germanic language, a subdivision of the Germanic languages that also includes English, Dutch, Afrikaans and Yiddish.

  • Paul / Langfocus: The German Language (YouTube, 12 minutes, 06.04.2016).
  • Goethe-Institut: Was müssen wir für die deutsche Sprache tun, Herr Ebert? (YouTube, 13 minutes, 11.12.2013).
  • L'atout des langues: Pourquoi apprendre l'allemand et non l'espagnol (YouTube, 11 minutes, 02.11.2017).

General Resources for Learning German

  • BBC: GCSE Bitesize German.
  • German Language Stack Exchange is a bilingual question and answer site for speakers of all levels who want to share and increase their knowledge of the German language.

  • Deutsche Welle (DW): Deutsch lernen: YouTube channel with dozens of videos to help you learn German. Many of the playlists also mention which CEFR level the videos are aimed at. Especially level B2/C1 has many playlists. For example, the YouTube channel has three playlists for the three seasons of the telenovela Jojo sucht das Glück: Staffel 1 (33 Videos), Staffel 2 (35 Videos), Staffel 3 (33 Videos).

    Deutsche Welle is Germany's international broadcaster. The section Learn German on its website contains language courses and other resources for learning German, for example, news broadcast that are spoken at a slower pace.
    Alltagsdeutsch is another useful section of their site that focuses on everyday language.
  • German at Work Post-Beginners 1: Deutsch am Arbeitsplatz is an online course created by The Open University in the UK and hosted on the MOOC platform FutureLearn. The course is four weeks long and requires five hours of study per week. The course was developed with support from YFEJ, a project that received financial support from the European Union Programme for Employment and Social Innovation "EaSI" (2014-2020).   • Deutsch für Euch: YouTube channel that teaches German. The level is elementary to intermediate; the language of instruction in English.
  • Deutsch Global: YouTube channel that teaches German. The level is elementary to intermediate; the language of instruction is German.
  • Deutsch Perfekt is the website of a monthly magazine for learning German. The magazine contains articles on three levels (leicht, mittel & schwer), and each article has a short vocabulary list with German explanations. For learners who still need translations for the articles, the magazine also contains a vocabulary table with translations in English, French, Spanish, Italian, Hungarian, Polish and Russian. The magazine also contains a section with exercises where you can practice some of the vocabulary and grammar used in the articles. The magazine's website contains additional materials (audio files and exercises); some of these materials are only accessible to subscribers of the magazine.

Spelling

The relationship between German spelling and pronunciation is relatively straighforward, but punctuation is different from other Germanic languages such as English and Dutch.

Language Tests and Certificates

Test Deutsch als Fremdsprache (TestDaF)

TestDaF is a standardised proficiency test for non-native speakers of German who want to study at or work in a German university. The test covers four skills: reading, listening, writing and speaking.

  • P. Moua: How to Pass the TestDAF - Tips and Resources You Need (YouTube, 6 minutes): basic tips for learners who are new to TestDAF.
  • TestDaF Institut: Vorbereitung auf die Prüfung.
    Tips and materials to prepare for the test.
  • Deutsch-Uni Online (DUO): uni-deutsch TestDaFtraining.
    An online course that requires level B2 (according to the CEFR) before you start.
  • godaf: a website that provides practice tests.
  • Longua: TestDaF - Tipps zu den Prüfungsteilen - Hören - Lesen - Schreiben - Mündlich.
  • TestDaf: YouTube playlist with 12 videos by Ekaterina Bazarova. The videos can be used to practice listening skills and increase vocabulary.
  • TestDaF Mündlicher Ausdruck: YouTube playlist with 7 tests.

The following resources are not directly related to TestDaF but skills and exercises that are part of the exam.

Großes Deutsches Sprachdiplom (Goethe-Institut)

The Großes Deutsches Sprachdiplom is the highest level exam offered by the Goethe-Institut. Its level corresponds to C2 in the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFL) and requires a near-native level of German.

  • Goethe-Institut: Goethe-Zertifikat C2: GDS: this page introduces the certificate, its goals and where it is recognised. The page also contains links to downloads (for example, to a document that describes the exam) and to pages that help you prepare for the exam.
    See also:
    • Practice materials and another page with practice materials.
    • Voraussetzungen.
    • Module: the exam consists of four modules: reading, listening, writing and speaking. The modules reading, listening and writing need to be finished within a timeframe of 195 minutes. The oral part is 15 minutes long.
  • Goethe Zertifikat C2: GDS Mündliche Prüfung (YouTube, 20 minutes): example of the oral part of the exam. This video was uploaded by the Goethe-Institut in Egypt.
  • Université de Lausanne: Einige Ratschläge für die Vorbereitung: some links for the preparation for the language exams organised by the Goethe-Institut.

Dictionaries

  • Duden: dictionary that provides the following types in information for almost every word in its database: grammatical information (e.g. grammatical gender), frequency, meaning(s), synonym(s), pronunciation, origin or etymology (if known), examples.
  • PONS Online-Wörterbuch: to access the learner's dictionary, select “Deutsch als Fremdsprache” from the language menu next to the search field.
  • Langenscheidt: translating dictionaries.
  • MundMische: Wörterbuch der deutschen Umgangssprache: a dictionary of colloquial German. The entries are created by volunteers; anyone can register to add definitions.
  • Wörterbuch Deutsch describes itself as a dictionary with definitions, usage examples, synonyms, translations, trends, phrases, etcetera. Reading / Literature

  • Simple but interesting German literature is a question on German Language Stack Exhchange with many suggestions for books that should be fairly easy to read for language learners.
  • Deutsch mit Marija: Sprachen lernen | Tipp #4 | In einer Fremdsprache lesen: 9-minute YouTube video about reading in a foreign language. Marija does not like abridged or graded readers because the changes to the text often remove its charm. Instead, she recommends reading a translation and the original work in parallel. This reduces the number of dictionary lookups without reducing the reading pleasure. In German, there are also a books that contain the original text and a translation on facing pages, for example by the German publisher dtv. Some children's books are also appropriate for learning languages, e.g. Astrid Lindgren's works, the Harry Potter novels, etcetera.
